Unit 1

Basic Economic Concepts

Scarcity, opportunity cost, the PPC, comparative advantage, and how economies organize production.

Scarcity & Opportunity Cost

Scarcity is the fundamental problem of economics: unlimited wants vs. limited resources. Because of scarcity, every choice has a cost.

Opportunity cost = the value of the next-best alternative you give up when you make a choice. It is ALWAYS a ratio when comparing production of two goods.

Opportunity Cost of Good A = (Amount of B given up) ÷ (Amount of A gained)

Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)

The PPC shows the maximum combinations of two goods an economy can produce using all its resources efficiently.

  • On the curve = productively efficient (full employment)
  • Inside the curve = inefficient / unemployment
  • Outside the curve = unattainable with current resources
  • Bowed-out shape = increasing opportunity cost (resources aren't equally suited to both goods)
  • Straight line = constant opportunity cost
  • Outward shift = economic growth (more resources, better technology, education)
Key Takeaway: The PPC can shift outward for only ONE good if technological improvement is industry-specific. A general improvement (education, capital investment) shifts the entire curve outward.
Key Takeaway: Moving from inside to a point ON the curve is NOT economic growth — it's reducing inefficiency. Growth means the curve itself shifts outward.

Key Graph: Production Possibilities Curve

Bowed-out PPC with economic growth shown as an outward shift (PPC1 → PPC2).

Comparative & Absolute Advantage

Absolute advantage: produce MORE with the same resources.

Comparative advantage: produce at a LOWER opportunity cost. This is the basis for specialization and trade.

Terms of trade must fall BETWEEN the two parties' opportunity costs for both to benefit.
Key Takeaway: A country CAN have absolute advantage in both goods but CANNOT have comparative advantage in both. If you have CA in one good, the other party has CA in the other. Always.
Key Takeaway: For output problems, the OC formula is "other good ÷ this good." For input problems (hours/resources needed), it flips: "this good ÷ other good." Watch out for which type the problem gives you!
AP Tip: Comparative advantage tables appear on almost every AP exam. Practice computing OC from BOTH output tables and input tables.

Economic Systems & Circular Flow

The circular flow model shows interactions between households and firms in product and factor markets.

  • Product market: Firms sell goods/services to households (GDP is measured here)
  • Factor (resource) market: Households sell labor, land, capital to firms
  • Leakages: saving, taxes, imports (money leaving the spending stream)
  • Injections: investment, government spending, exports (money entering)
Key Takeaway: In equilibrium, leakages = injections. If injections > leakages, GDP rises. If leakages > injections, GDP falls.

Practice Problems — Unit 1

Q1
Country X produces 100 cars or 200 computers. Country Y produces 60 cars or 180 computers. Which is correct?
Answer: C. X's OC of 1 computer = 100/200 = 0.5 cars. Y's OC of 1 computer = 60/180 = 0.33 cars. Y has lower OC → comparative advantage in computers.
Q2
A point inside the PPC represents:
Answer: B. Inside the PPC = not using all resources efficiently. Could be unemployment, underutilized factories, or poor allocation.
Q3
Which would shift the PPC outward?
Answer: D. Technology that improves ALL industries shifts the entire PPC outward. Reduced unemployment moves toward the curve (not a shift). Spending & surpluses don't change productive capacity.
Q4
In Country A, the opportunity cost of 1 unit of wheat is 3 units of cloth. In Country B, the opportunity cost of 1 unit of wheat is 2 units of cloth. Which statement is TRUE?
Answer: B. Country B gives up only 2 cloth per wheat vs. A's 3. B has lower OC of wheat → CA in wheat. A has CA in cloth (A: OC of 1 cloth = 1/3 wheat vs B: OC of 1 cloth = 1/2 wheat; 1/3 < 1/2).
Q5
Which best describes a straight-line PPC?
Answer: C. A straight-line PPC means resources are perfectly adaptable to producing either good, so the trade-off ratio stays the same at every point.
Unit 2

Measuring Economic Performance

GDP, unemployment types, inflation, price indices, the business cycle, and economic indicators.

Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP = total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a given year.

Expenditure Approach: GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)

C = consumer spending (~70%) | I = gross private investment | G = government spending (NOT transfers) | X−M = net exports

Income Approach: GDP = wages + rent + interest + profits
Key Takeaway: GDP does NOT include: used goods, intermediate goods, financial transactions (stocks/bonds), transfer payments (Social Security, welfare), non-market activities (housework), or underground economy.
Key Takeaway: Investment (I) in GDP means business investment — new factories, equipment, construction, inventory changes. It does NOT mean buying stocks/bonds.

Nominal vs. Real GDP

GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP ÷ Real GDP) × 100
Real GDP = (Nominal GDP ÷ GDP Deflator) × 100

Nominal GDP can rise just because prices rose. Real GDP adjusts for inflation and shows true output changes.

Key Takeaway: In the BASE YEAR, Nominal GDP = Real GDP, and the GDP Deflator = 100. If the deflator is 120, prices are 20% higher than the base year.
AP Tip: Per capita real GDP (Real GDP ÷ population) is the best measure of living standards over time. Total real GDP alone doesn't account for population growth.

Unemployment

Unemployment Rate = (# Unemployed ÷ Labor Force) × 100
Labor Force = Employed + Unemployed (actively seeking work)
LFPR = (Labor Force ÷ Working-Age Population) × 100

Three types:

  • Frictional — between jobs, new entrants, voluntary transitions. ALWAYS exists.
  • Structural — skills mismatch, tech change, geographic mismatch. Can be long-lasting.
  • Cyclical — caused by recessions (insufficient demand).
Natural Rate of Unemployment (NRU) = Frictional + Structural (ZERO cyclical)
Key Takeaway: "Full employment" does NOT mean 0% unemployment. It means zero CYCLICAL unemployment. Frictional and structural are considered natural.
Key Takeaway: Discouraged workers, part-time workers who want full-time, and people not actively searching are NOT in the labor force. This means the official rate understates true joblessness.

Inflation & Price Indices

CPI = (Cost of basket in current year ÷ Cost in base year) × 100
Inflation Rate = [(CPI2 − CPI1) ÷ CPI1] × 100
Fisher Equation: Real Interest Rate = Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate

Costs of inflation: Menu costs, shoe-leather costs, uncertainty, income redistribution. Unexpected inflation hurts lenders/savers, helps borrowers.

Key Takeaway: The REAL interest rate is what matters for economic decisions. If nominal rate = 6% and inflation = 4%, savers only earn 2% in purchasing power.
Key Takeaway: Deflation (falling prices) can be worse than moderate inflation — it increases the real burden of debt, discourages spending (wait for lower prices), and can create a deflationary spiral.

Key Graph: The Business Cycle

Expansion → Peak → Contraction → Trough, cycling around the long-run growth trend.

Unit 3

Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply

The core macro model — AD, SRAS, LRAS, equilibrium, output gaps, the multiplier, and self-correction.

Aggregate Demand (AD)

AD = total spending at each price level. Slopes downward due to three effects:

  • Wealth effect: Lower PL → higher real wealth → more C
  • Interest rate effect: Lower PL → less money demand → lower i → more I
  • Net export effect: Lower PL → domestic goods cheaper abroad → more Xn

Shifters of AD: Anything that changes C, I, G, or Xn NOT caused by a price level change.

Key Takeaway: A change in the PRICE LEVEL causes movement ALONG AD. A change in a component of spending (consumer confidence, government policy, etc.) SHIFTS AD.

Short-Run & Long-Run Aggregate Supply

SRAS slopes upward — as PL rises, sticky wages mean profit margins increase and firms produce more.

LRAS is vertical at potential GDP (Yf) — in the long run, wages fully adjust and output depends only on resources & technology.

SRAS shifters: Input prices (wages, oil), productivity, supply shocks, taxes/subsidies on producers, inflationary expectations.

LRAS shifters: Changes in quantity/quality of resources, technology, institutions (anything that changes potential GDP).

Key Takeaway: If ONLY AD shifts, PL and real GDP move in the SAME direction. If ONLY SRAS shifts, PL and real GDP move in OPPOSITE directions (this is what makes supply shocks so problematic).
Key Takeaway: LRAS shifts are the SAME things that shift the PPC. Both represent the economy's productive capacity.

Key Graph: AD-AS Model (Recessionary Gap)

AD intersects SRAS to the LEFT of LRAS → recessionary gap (Y1 < Yf).

Long-Run Self-Correction

Recessionary gap: High unemployment → wages fall → SRAS shifts RIGHT → return to Yf at lower PL.

Inflationary gap: Low unemployment → wages rise → SRAS shifts LEFT → return to Yf at higher PL.

Key Takeaway: Self-correction works through WAGE ADJUSTMENT shifting SRAS. It does NOT involve AD shifting. The long-run equilibrium is where AD, SRAS, and LRAS all meet.
Key Takeaway: Self-correction can be very slow (especially from a recession), which is the argument FOR active fiscal/monetary policy intervention.

The Multiplier Effect

MPC + MPS = 1
Spending Multiplier = 1 ÷ MPS = 1 ÷ (1 − MPC)
Tax Multiplier = −MPC ÷ MPS
Balanced Budget Multiplier = 1 (always)
ΔReal GDP = Multiplier × Initial ΔSpending
Key Takeaway: The spending multiplier is ALWAYS larger (in absolute value) than the tax multiplier. Why? The first dollar of government spending is fully spent. The first dollar of a tax cut is only partially spent (MPC) and partially saved (MPS).
Key Takeaway: The balanced budget multiplier is always exactly 1. If G rises by $100 and T rises by $100, GDP increases by exactly $100.

Practice Problems — Unit 3

Q12

Answer: B. 1/(1−0.8) = 1/0.2 = 5.

A negative supply shock (oil price spike) causes:
Answer: A. Higher input costs → SRAS shifts left → stagflation (prices up, output down). Policymaker's nightmare.

Answer: C. High unemployment → wages fall → lower production costs → SRAS shifts RIGHT → economy returns to Yf at a lower PL.

Answer: C. Multiplier = 5. ΔGDP = 5 × $50B = $250B.

Answer: B. Tax multiplier = −0.8/0.2 = −4. ΔGDP = −4 × (−$50B) = +$200B. Note: smaller effect than the same $50B in spending.

Answer: A. Lower confidence → consumers spend less → C falls → AD shifts LEFT.

Unit 4

Fiscal Policy

Government taxing & spending tools, automatic stabilizers, the crowding-out effect, and deficits.

Fiscal Policy Tools

Expansionary (fight recession): ↑G or ↓T → AD shifts RIGHT.

Contractionary (fight inflation): ↓G or ↑T → AD shifts LEFT.

Key Takeaway: Fiscal policy is conducted by Congress and the President — NOT the Fed. It has implementation lags because it requires legislative action.
Key Takeaway: Changes in G shift AD by the FULL spending multiplier. Tax changes shift AD by the smaller tax multiplier (because part of the tax change is saved, not spent).

Automatic Stabilizers

Built-in budget features that cushion the business cycle without new legislation:

  • Progressive income taxes: Revenues fall in recessions (disposable income cushioned); rise in booms (restraining spending).
  • Unemployment benefits: Payments increase in recessions; decrease in expansions.
Key Takeaway: Automatic stabilizers reduce the SEVERITY of cycles but cannot eliminate them. They work FASTER than discretionary policy because they don't require Congressional action.

Crowding-Out Effect

Government borrows to fund deficit spending → demand for loanable funds ↑ → real interest rate ↑ → private investment ↓.

Gov't deficit spending → Borrow in LF market → Real i rises → Private I falls → Partially offsets stimulus
Key Takeaway: Crowding out means the ACTUAL GDP increase is LESS than what the multiplier alone would predict. The spending multiplier gives the MAXIMUM possible effect.
Key Takeaway: The crowding out debate: Keynesians argue it's small in a recession (lots of idle savings). Classical economists argue it's large and makes fiscal policy ineffective.

Key Graph: Loanable Funds Market (Crowding Out)

Government borrowing shifts D right (D1→D2), raising real interest rate (r1→r2) and crowding out private investment.

Practice Problems — Unit 4

Q18
MPC = 0.8. Government CUTS taxes by $100B. Max ΔGDP:
Answer: D. Tax multiplier = −0.8/0.2 = −4. ΔGDP = −4 × (−$100B) = +$400B.
Q19
The crowding-out effect means expansionary fiscal policy:
Answer: B. Government borrowing raises interest rates, reducing private investment. The stimulus is partially offset.
Q20
Which is an automatic stabilizer?
Answer: C. Progressive taxes automatically adjust without legislation: revenues fall in recessions, rise in booms.
Q21
Government increases G by $100B AND raises taxes by $100B. GDP changes by:
Answer: B. Balanced budget multiplier = 1. GDP rises by exactly the amount of the equal spending/tax change.
Q22
To fight demand-pull inflation, the government should:
Answer: A. Contractionary fiscal policy: decrease G or increase T → AD shifts left → PL falls, closing the inflationary gap. Options C and D are monetary policy (Fed, not Congress).
Unit 5

The Financial Sector

Money, banking, fractional reserves, money creation, and the money market.

Functions & Measures of Money

Three functions: Medium of exchange, unit of account, store of value.

M1 = currency + checking deposits + traveler's checks (most liquid). M2 = M1 + savings + CDs + money market funds.

Key Takeaway: Credit cards are NOT money — they're a short-term loan. Debit cards access checking deposits (M1). Stocks and bonds are NOT money.

Fractional Reserve Banking & Money Creation

Required Reserves = Deposit × Reserve Ratio (RR)
Excess Reserves = Deposit − Required Reserves
Money Multiplier = 1 ÷ Reserve Ratio
Max ΔMoney Supply = Money Multiplier × Initial Excess Reserves
Key Takeaway: Individual banks can only lend out their EXCESS reserves. The BANKING SYSTEM as a whole creates money through the multiplier process as deposits are re-lent.
Key Takeaway: The money multiplier gives the MAXIMUM possible money creation. In reality, money creation is less if banks hold excess reserves or people hold cash.
AP Tip: A common trick: "How much can BANK A lend?" = its excess reserves. "How much NEW money can the BANKING SYSTEM create?" = multiplier × excess reserves.

The Money Market

Money Supply (MS) is vertical — set by the Fed, independent of interest rate.

Money Demand (MD) slopes downward — lower interest rate = lower opportunity cost of holding cash = people hold more money.

MD shifters: Changes in price level (higher PL → more money needed → MD right); changes in real GDP/income.

Key Takeaway: The money market determines the NOMINAL interest rate. When MS increases, the interest rate FALLS. When MS decreases, the interest rate RISES.
Key Takeaway: If interest rate is above equilibrium: surplus of money → people buy bonds → bond prices rise → interest rate falls back to equilibrium. The bond price and interest rate move in OPPOSITE directions.

Key Graph: The Money Market

Fed increases money supply (MS1→MS2) → nominal interest rate falls (i1→i2).

Practice Problems — Unit 5

Q23
Reserve ratio = 20%. New deposit of $5,000. Max NEW money the banking SYSTEM creates:
Answer: C. Multiplier = 1/0.20 = 5. Total deposits = 5 × $5,000 = $25,000. NEW money = $25,000 − $5,000 = $20,000.
Q24
An increase in the price level will:
Answer: A. Higher PL → more money needed for transactions → MD shifts RIGHT → interest rate rises.
Q25
Reserve ratio = 25%. Bank A receives $10,000 deposit. Bank A can lend out:
Answer: C. Required reserves = $10,000 × 0.25 = $2,500. Excess = $10,000 − $2,500 = $7,500. One bank lends only its excess.
Q26
Bond prices and interest rates are:
Answer: B. When people buy bonds, bond prices rise and interest rates fall. When people sell bonds, prices fall and interest rates rise. Always inversely related.
Unit 6

Monetary Policy

The Federal Reserve's tools, the transmission mechanism, the Phillips Curve, and policy trade-offs.

The Fed's Monetary Policy Tools

  • Open Market Operations (OMO) — Buy bonds = expansionary (↑MS, ↓i). Sell bonds = contractionary (↓MS, ↑i). Primary tool.
  • Discount Rate — Rate Fed charges banks. ↓ = expansionary. ↑ = contractionary.
  • Reserve Requirement — ↓RR = more lending = expansionary. ↑RR = less lending = contractionary.
  • Federal Funds Rate (FFR) — Overnight interbank rate. The Fed targets this via OMO.
Key Takeaway: Monetary policy is conducted by the Federal Reserve (NOT Congress). It can be implemented faster than fiscal policy because the Fed doesn't need legislative approval.
Key Takeaway: The Fed targets the federal funds rate, NOT the interest rate directly. By buying/selling bonds, the Fed changes bank reserves which moves the FFR toward the target.

The Chain of Causation — KNOW THIS COLD

EXPANSIONARY: Fed buys bonds → bank reserves ↑ → MS ↑ → nominal i ↓ → I ↑ (and C ↑) → AD → → real GDP ↑, PL ↑, unemployment ↓
CONTRACTIONARY: Fed sells bonds → bank reserves ↓ → MS ↓ → nominal i ↑ → I ↓ (and C ↓) → AD ← → real GDP ↓, PL ↓, unemployment ↑
Key Takeaway: On FRQs, you MUST show EVERY LINK. Don't skip from "Fed buys bonds" to "GDP increases." Show: MS → interest rate → investment → AD → GDP/PL. Missing a link = missing a point.
Key Takeaway: Monetary policy affects AD INDIRECTLY through interest rates. Fiscal policy affects AD DIRECTLY (G) or semi-directly (T → disposable income → C).
AP Tip: The Fed CANNOT control both the money supply and the interest rate independently. If it targets the interest rate, it must let MS adjust. If it targets MS, the interest rate moves freely.

The Phillips Curve

Short-Run Phillips Curve (SRPC): Inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. Moving ALONG the SRPC reflects changes in AD.

Long-Run Phillips Curve (LRPC): Vertical at the NRU. No long-run trade-off between inflation and unemployment.

  • ↑AD → move UP and LEFT along SRPC (lower unemployment, higher inflation)
  • ↓AD → move DOWN and RIGHT along SRPC (higher unemployment, lower inflation)
  • Supply shock or change in expected inflation → SRPC SHIFTS
  • Negative supply shock → SRPC shifts RIGHT/UP (stagflation)
  • Positive supply shock → SRPC shifts LEFT/DOWN
Key Takeaway: The SRPC is the mirror image of AD-AS. When AD shifts right in AD-AS (higher GDP, higher PL), the economy moves up-left on the Phillips Curve (lower unemployment, higher inflation).
Key Takeaway: LRAS is vertical at Yf. LRPC is vertical at NRU. They represent the same concept: the economy's long-run equilibrium output and employment.
Key Takeaway: When expected inflation changes, the SRPC shifts. If workers expect 5% inflation and negotiate higher wages, the SRPC shifts up — at every unemployment rate, actual inflation is higher.

Key Graph: Phillips Curve

A = long-run equilibrium. B = expansionary policy (up-left along SRPC). C = contractionary (down-right).

Unit 7

International Trade & Finance

Balance of payments, exchange rates, capital flows, and the international effects of domestic policy.

Balance of Payments

Current Account: Trade in goods & services, investment income, net transfers. A trade DEFICIT = imports > exports.

Capital (Financial) Account: Investment flows (FDI, portfolio investment).

Current Account + Capital Account = 0 (they must balance)
Key Takeaway: A current account DEFICIT must be offset by a capital account SURPLUS. The country is buying more than it sells, financed by foreign investment inflows.
Key Takeaway: The U.S. has run a persistent current account deficit for decades — this means foreigners are investing heavily in U.S. assets (buying Treasury bonds, real estate, stocks).

Exchange Rates

Appreciation: Currency gets stronger → imports cheaper, exports more expensive → net exports ↓.

Depreciation: Currency gets weaker → imports more expensive, exports cheaper → net exports ↑.

Demand for a currency increases (appreciation) when:

  • Foreigners buy our exports
  • Foreign investors invest in our country (buy our assets)
  • Our interest rates rise relative to others (capital inflows)

Supply of a currency increases (depreciation) when:

  • We buy imports from abroad
  • We invest in foreign countries
  • Our interest rates fall (capital outflows)
  • Our inflation rises relative to others
Key Takeaway: INTEREST RATE DIFFERENTIALS drive exchange rates in the short run. If U.S. rates rise, foreign capital pours in → demand for $ rises → $ appreciates.
Key Takeaway: INFLATION DIFFERENTIALS drive exchange rates in the long run. If U.S. inflation is higher, our goods get expensive → fewer exports → $ depreciates.

The Critical International Linkage Chain

↑ US interest rates → capital flows IN → D$ ↑ → $ APPRECIATES → US exports ↓, imports ↑ → Xn ↓ → AD ↓
↓ US interest rates → capital flows OUT → S$ ↑ → $ DEPRECIATES → US exports ↑, imports ↓ → Xn ↑ → AD ↑
Key Takeaway: This is the MOST TESTED chain in Unit 7. Contractionary monetary policy is REINFORCED by the trade effect: higher rates directly reduce I, AND the stronger dollar reduces Xn. Double hit to AD.
Key Takeaway: Expansionary monetary policy is also reinforced: lower rates increase I domestically, AND the weaker dollar boosts Xn. Double boost to AD.
AP Tip: Know how to draw the forex market. Supply and demand for a currency, with the exchange rate (price of $ in foreign currency) on the Y-axis. Shifts in D$ or S$ change the exchange rate.

Key Graph: Foreign Exchange Market (USD)

Higher U.S. interest rates → D$ increases (D$1→D$2) → dollar appreciates (e1→e2).
